## 4. Universal rules — patterns appearing in ≥ 3 papers regardless of genre

These are the rules that recurred across genres. They form the **core playbook** — apply them to every paper you write, regardless of what you are writing.

> [!success] Universal rules: the cross-genre playbook
>
> 1. **Hedge causes, not measurements (Lipton's discrimination).** Direct verbs for what you measured ("we observe X"); modal verbs for *why* X happened ("X may be explained by Y", "possible explanations include..."). The asymmetry is the credibility signal — reviewers reward calibrated language on mechanism and punish hedging on data. Cited in: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Bonnaire-2025|Bonnaire #7]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Chase-2025|Chase #7]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Karras-2024|Karras #7]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Liu-2025|Liu #6]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Qiu-2025|Qiu #9]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Tian-2024|Tian #9]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Wang-2025|Wang #10]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Yue-2025|Yue #9]]. **8/11 papers.**
>
> 2. **Captions are claims, not legends.** Every figure caption ends with a sentence that asserts the thesis the figure supports. Generic openers like "Plot of...", "Heatmap showing...", "Schematic of..." indicate a wasted caption. The test: if all your figure captions were removed, the reader should lose information; they should not just lose labels. Cited in: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Bonnaire-2025|Bonnaire #6]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Karras-2024|Karras #4]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Liu-2025|Liu #5]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Tian-2024|Tian #7]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Wang-2025|Wang #7]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Artificial-Hivemind|Hivemind §11.2]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Qiu-2025|Qiu §11.2]]. **7/11 papers.**
>
> 3. **Anchor 5-7 numbers and repeat them verbatim.** Pick a small set of numbers (headline metric, scale, comparator ratio, model size) and place each in abstract + intro + figure + table + conclusion. Never paraphrase ("about thirty thousand" → "30k"). Numbers seen four times feel like rigor; numbers seen once feel like decoration. Cited in: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Artificial-Hivemind|Hivemind #9]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Bonnaire-2025|Bonnaire #2]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Chase-2025|Chase #1]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Karras-2024|Karras #5]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Lin-2024|Lin #7]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Liu-2025|Liu #4]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Tian-2024|Tian #6]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Wang-2025|Wang #4]]. **8/11 papers.**
>
> 4. **The title should encode the contribution.** Whether the contribution is a phenomenon ("Artificial Hivemind"), a finding list ("Non-linearity, Sparsity, and Attention-Sink-Free"), a question ("Does RL Really Incentivize..."), a counterintuition ("...with a Bad Version of Itself"), a scaling claim ("1000 Layer Networks"), or a mechanism ("Visual Autoregressive Modeling: ... via Next-Scale Prediction") — the title must do *some* rhetorical work. A title that is only the method shortname leaves value on the table. Cited in: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Karras-2024|Karras #1]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Lin-2024|Lin #1]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Liu-2025|Liu #1]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Qiu-2025|Qiu #1]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Tian-2024|Tian #1]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Wang-2025|Wang #1]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Yue-2025|Yue #1]]. **7/11 papers.**
>
> 5. **Two scientific legs on one artifact.** Top-venue papers nearly always have ≥ 2 distinct findings supported by one core contribution. The dataset paper has *generation* + *evaluation* legs; the mechanism paper has *what improved* + *why it improved*; the theory paper has *upper bound* + *matching lower bound*; the empirical paper has *real-world measurement* + *toy-model theory*. A single-leg paper is rejected as "incremental." Cited in: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Artificial-Hivemind|Hivemind #2]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Bonnaire-2025|Bonnaire #3]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Lin-2024|Lin #6]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Qiu-2025|Qiu #3]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Tian-2024|Tian #3]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Wang-2025|Wang #8]]. **6/11 papers (implicit in others).**
>
> 6. **Steelman the obvious objection inside the section it addresses, not in Limitations.** Every paper in the corpus pre-empts at least one obvious reviewer rebuttal *in body text*, not in an afterthought. Mechanisms: in-section sub-experiments (Hivemind: min-p), parameter-equalised baselines (Qiu), causal-isolation experiments (Wang, Qiu), positive controls (Yue: distillation), negative theorems (Shi), pre-emptive sub-section headings (Bonnaire), domain-parallel experiments (Lin). Cited in: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Artificial-Hivemind|Hivemind #8]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Bonnaire-2025|Bonnaire #5]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Liu-2025|Liu #8]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Qiu-2025|Qiu #6, #7]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Shi-2024|Shi #7]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Tian-2024|Tian #5]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Wang-2025|Wang #9]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Yue-2025|Yue #8]]. **8/11 papers.**
>
> 7. **Open the abstract with the specific problem, not the field.** *Avoid*: "Large language models have achieved remarkable success in..." / "Gating mechanisms have been widely utilized..." (any sentence that could open every paper in the field). *Prefer*: open with the problem ("LMs fail to produce diverse content..."), the asymmetry ("Scaling worked in X, not Z"), the cost expression ("Computing kth-order derivatives scales as $O(d^k)$..."), or the specific practice being challenged ("Previous methods have uniformly applied..."). Cited in: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Artificial-Hivemind|Hivemind §2]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Lin-2024|Lin #3]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Shi-2024|Shi #3]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Wang-2025|Wang #3]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Yue-2025|Yue #2]]. **5/11 papers (Qiu and Bonnaire flagged as exhibiting the soft anti-pattern).**
>
> 8. **Italicise / quote your thesis sentence in the abstract.** A copy-pasteable thesis sentence is what reviewers lift into their reviews. Make it one sentence, in italics or quotation marks, so it visually separates from the surrounding prose. Cited in: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Artificial-Hivemind|Hivemind #7]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Lin-2024|Lin #2]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Yue-2025|Yue #3]]. **3/11 papers explicit; many others use italics on key phrases.**
>
> 9. **Section headers should be claims, not topics.** *"Selected Token Loss Aligns Better with Downstream Performance"* (Lin) and *"Deep Networks Unlock Batch Size Scaling"* (Wang) tell a skimmer the finding. *"Token Selection Analysis"* and *"Batch Size"* do not. A reader reading only your H2/H3 list should know your contributions. Cited in: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Lin-2024|Lin #8]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Wang-2025|Wang #6]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Liu-2025|Liu #3]] (Result boxes with quotable names), [[Writing-Best-Practices-Bonnaire-2025|Bonnaire #5]] (sub-headings as rebuttals). **4/11 papers explicit.**
>
> 10. **Conclusion is for compression — ≤ 10-25 lines, no new content.** Every paper in the corpus has a short conclusion. The longest is Karras at ~25 lines. None introduces new figures, numbers, or claims. The conclusion restates: (a) the named artifact, (b) the named phenomenon/finding, (c) one stake (social or scientific). Chase goes one step further: *skip the conclusion entirely* in favour of an open-questions list — viable when the theorem statement and Q-staircase already encapsulate the contribution. Cited in: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Artificial-Hivemind|Hivemind §9]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Chase-2025|Chase #9]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Karras-2024|Karras #9]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Qiu-2025|Qiu §9]]. **All papers obey to varying degrees.**
>
> 11. **Negative results and "we tried this and it didn't work" disclosures buy credibility.** The corpus uses three placements: main-body section (Wang §4.6 "Does X improve Y?" with answer *no*), appendix subsection (Qiu A.5: failed clipping; Shi: "why obvious alternative X doesn't help"), or pre-emptive listed *not-used* techniques (Tian: RoPE, SwiGLU, RMSNorm explicitly listed as deliberately omitted). Reviewers reward honesty about boundaries. Cited in: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Qiu-2025|Qiu #8]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Shi-2024|Shi #9]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Tian-2024|Tian #5]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Wang-2025|Wang #9]]. **4/11 papers explicit.**
>
> 12. **Appendix as reviewer insurance — every body claim has an appendix slot.** Universal pattern: every main-paper "we did X" is backed by an appendix that documents X in full. The discipline varies by genre (prompts + human validation for datasets; negative-result experiments for architecture; mirrored proofs for theory; per-domain robustness for empirical), but the shape is universal. Cited in: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Artificial-Hivemind|Hivemind #10]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Chase-2025|Chase #3]] (Technical Overview), [[Writing-Best-Practices-Liu-2025|Liu #7]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Shi-2024|Shi #9]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Tian-2024|Tian #8]]. **5/11 papers explicit; all 11 implicit.**

## 5. Genre-specific rules

When starting a new paper, look up the section matching your dominant genre. These are rules that *did not* recur across genres in our corpus and are therefore likely genre-conditional.

### 5.1 Dataset / phenomenon papers
*(applies when: you are publishing a dataset, benchmark, or naming a new phenomenon. Canonical example: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Artificial-Hivemind|Jiang 2025, Artificial Hivemind]].)*

> [!tip] Genre-specific playbook
> 1. **Name *both* the phenomenon *and* the resource with distinct typography.** A dataset paper without a brand is harder to cite. The Hivemind paper has two brands ("Artificial Hivemind" the phenomenon + "I**NFINITY**-C**HAT**" the resource) and uses them in every section. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Artificial-Hivemind|Hivemind #1]]. *Genre-specific because architecture papers usually use positional conventions (G_1...G_5) instead of branded entities; theory papers use anchor numbers ($\sqrt{d}$).*
> 2. **Hero Figure 1 must include *verbatim model outputs / data instances*, not just aggregate scores.** Real model names, actual generated text, color-coded annotations. The figure carries the thesis. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Artificial-Hivemind|Hivemind #3]]. *Genre-specific because architecture papers' hero figures show the mechanism diagram + a result curve; theory papers show a proof sketch or phase diagram.*
> 3. **Quantitative + qualitative evidence on every claim.** Every aggregate similarity score gets a pasted example next to it. Without qualitative anchoring, dataset claims feel abstract. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Artificial-Hivemind|Hivemind #6]]. *Less universal because architecture papers can lean entirely on numerical tables once the mechanism is established.*
> 4. **LLM-labelled / LLM-filtered data needs a human-validation study in the appendix.** Verbatim prompts + N-participant Prolific study + agreement statistics. Without this, the entire dataset depends on un-verified trust in the LLM filter. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Artificial-Hivemind|Hivemind #10]]. *Genre-specific because non-dataset papers don't usually use LLM labelling pipelines as the load-bearing component.*

### 5.2 Architecture / mechanism papers
*(applies when: you are proposing a new architecture component, training technique, or empirical mechanism. Canonical examples: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Qiu-2025|Qiu]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Karras-2024|Karras]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Shi-2024|Shi]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Tian-2024|Tian]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Lin-2024|Lin]].)*

> [!tip] Genre-specific playbook
> 1. **Positional / variant naming convention for ablation axes.** When ablating over a configuration space, label each variant (G_1, G_2, …) with semantic meaning, used in *every* figure, table, equation, prose mention. Turns a 15-row ablation table into a navigable map. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Qiu-2025|Qiu #2]].
> 2. **Two-symbol contribution.** Identify the two symbols / quantities your method is the first to handle simultaneously (e.g. *d* and *k* in Shi 2024) and repeat the pair in every section. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Shi-2024|Shi #1]].
> 3. **Split experiments (what improved) and analysis (why it improved) into two separate top-level sections.** Each gets its own narrative arc; analysis subsections match the title's subtitle nouns. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Qiu-2025|Qiu #3]].
> 4. **Mechanism before measurement.** When your method replaces a prior method, write the prior method's mechanism section first. Your method then reads as the inevitable clean replacement. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Karras-2024|Karras #2]].
> 5. **Three evidence types per mechanism claim:** math + figure + table. Each targets a different reviewer skeptic. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Qiu-2025|Qiu #4]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Shi-2024|Shi #4]].
> 6. **Disclose deployment cost in setup, not in conclusion.** Italicised sentence: *"the wall-time latency introduced by [technique] is less than N%"*. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Qiu-2025|Qiu #5]].
> 7. **Parameter-equalised baselines.** Run baselines with the same param budget so the gain can't be attributed to scale alone. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Qiu-2025|Qiu #6]].
> 8. **Causal separation experiments.** Design experiments that *remove* the proposed mechanism while keeping everything else (RMSNorm without gating; NS-sigmoid without sparsity). Distinct from architecture ablations. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Qiu-2025|Qiu #7]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Wang-2025|Wang #8]].
> 9. **Diagnosis-as-preliminaries.** Write §Preliminaries as a chain of claim-named subsections that show why prior approaches don't suffice. By §Method, the gap has built itself. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Shi-2024|Shi #5]].
> 10. **Parallel §Methods structure: §M.1 = problems with old approach, §M.2 = fixes.** The parallelism is the argument. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Tian-2024|Tian #4]].
> 11. **Scaffold on a memorable analogy when one is available.** "GPT for images" (Tian: VAR ↔ LLM next-token). Port the figure idioms, section idioms, and number idioms — not just the technique. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Tian-2024|Tian #2]].
> 12. **Subsume + prove the moat.** If your method generalises a prior method, prove (a) the prior method is a special case, and (b) it cannot be patched to reach your regime. Both halves matter. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Shi-2024|Shi #7]].
> 13. **List the fashionable techniques you do NOT use.** Every named-and-rejected trick (RoPE, SwiGLU, RMSNorm) is an ablation you preemptively don't have to run. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Tian-2024|Tian #5]].
> 14. **Reproducibility is a shell snippet, not a promise.** An 8-line click-runnable algorithm in the appendix beats "code will be released." Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Karras-2024|Karras #8]].
> 15. **Pre-method observation section.** Devote one section to the empirical pathology your method responds to — with *named categories* whose percentages sum to 100. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Lin-2024|Lin #5]].

### 5.3 Empirical scaling papers
*(applies when: you are selling an empirical regularity — a curve, law, or scaling relation. Canonical examples: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Liu-2025|Liu]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Lin-2024|Lin]] (partially), [[Writing-Best-Practices-Wang-2025|Wang]] (partially).)*

> [!tip] Genre-specific playbook
> 1. **Two-regime taxonomy.** Define a small named axis (weak vs strong superposition, depth-saturated vs depth-unlocking) in §2 and label every later figure/equation/result by axis position. Two named regimes do more rhetorical work than five vague cases. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Liu-2025|Liu #2]].
> 2. **Coloured callout boxes for headline results.** Put each numbered Result in a coloured box with a quotable name ("Power law in, power law out") so reviewers can lift it into reviews without re-reading. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Liu-2025|Liu #3]].
> 3. **Bridge toy to real with a single anchor number.** Pick the quantity the toy predicts and the real system measures, then re-cite that number in abstract + caption + every Result box + conclusion. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Liu-2025|Liu #4]].
> 4. **Comparator ratios beat absolute numbers in the abstract.** "Matching DeepSeekMath with only **3% of the pretraining tokens**" beats "15B vs. 500B tokens". Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Lin-2024|Lin #4]].
> 5. **Causal-isolation experiment as second leg.** Hold one factor constant (data) while varying another (capacity); the licensed inference is then a *must*, not a *might*. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Wang-2025|Wang #8]].
> 6. **Bolded-label contribution bullets.** Each contribution is one label + one paragraph + one headline number. Reviewers cite labels, not sentences. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Wang-2025|Wang #5]].

### 5.4 Theory papers
*(applies when: you are proving a theorem, establishing an impossibility, or giving a tight bound. Canonical examples: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Chase-2025|Chase]], [[Writing-Best-Practices-Bonnaire-2025|Bonnaire]] (hybrid).)*

> [!tip] Genre-specific playbook
> 1. **Anchor on one closed-form number.** Your central quantity (e.g. $\sqrt{d}$) appears in the title's implicit claim, the abstract, the named theorem, an intuition section, every appendix subsection, and the open-questions list. The theory-paper equivalent of a benchmark headline number. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Chase-2025|Chase #1]].
> 2. **Numbered question staircase.** Question 1 (broad) → Question 2 (operationalized) → Theorem (formal answer). Number them so reviewers can cite by question number. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Chase-2025|Chase #2]].
> 3. **Technical Overview between Intro and Appendix.** Three layers: named theorem in §1, informal proof sketches in §2 (Technical Overview), rigorous proofs in appendix. Non-theory venue reviewers read only the first two. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Chase-2025|Chase #3]].
> 4. **Code-name every proof subroutine.** Replace "our adaptive procedure" with *danger zone minimization*. The brand is shorter, more quotable, and lets the appendix refer to *components*. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Chase-2025|Chase #4]].
> 5. **Mirror upper and lower bounds at every structural level.** Twin theorem-bullets, twin overview-subsections, twin appendices. Tightness should be visually obvious without being separately asserted. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Chase-2025|Chase #5]].
> 6. **Visualize the diff when two settings differ in one detail.** Boxed adjacent procedure blocks for "standard" vs "transductive" make the 3-line difference legible. Don't rely on prose to convey a diff. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Chase-2025|Chase #6]].
> 7. **Operationalize open problems.** Replace "future work could investigate efficiency" with a quasi-formal definition of "efficient" here. Every open question is a citable follow-up. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Chase-2025|Chase #8]].
> 8. **Phase diagram with named, colour-coded regions** (theory-empirical hybrid). Build the paper around one diagram; every later section is a guided tour of one region. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Bonnaire-2025|Bonnaire #1]].
> 9. **Single-symbol named scalars** ($\tau_\text{gen}$, $\tau_\text{mem}$). Define in the abstract. A scaling claim is unquotable without symbols. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Bonnaire-2025|Bonnaire #2]].
> 10. **Visually rhyme empirical and theoretical panels.** When pairing realistic numerics with a tractable model, the empirical figure's panel structure and the theory figure's panel structure should look like one another. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Bonnaire-2025|Bonnaire #3]].

### 5.5 Position / contrarian papers
*(applies when: you are reframing the field or defending a contrarian thesis. Canonical example: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Yue-2025|Yue]].)*

> [!tip] Genre-specific playbook
> 1. **Title as question, conclusion as answer.** Yes/no question in the title; declarative sentence in §Conclusion. A reader who reads only title + last paragraph still knows the finding. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Yue-2025|Yue #1]].
> 2. **Five-beat abstract: belief → instrument → inversion → contrast → so-what.** Cite the success supporting the belief; name the belief; introduce your instrument; state the inversion (in italics); name your contrast class. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Yue-2025|Yue #2]].
> 3. **Defend your instrument before you use it.** A position paper lives on its measurement. Pre-empt the three most likely "wrong metric" objections in §Preliminaries. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Yue-2025|Yue #4]].
> 4. **The positive control is structurally critical.** A "method X has a limit" claim requires an existence proof that ¬limit is achievable on the same axis (here: §4.2 on distillation). Without the contrast class, the limit looks like physics, not method-specific. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Yue-2025|Yue #7]].
> 5. **One §section per reviewer objection.** §Analysis is a sequence of "alternative explanation → controlled experiment that closes the door." If a reviewer can think of a confound you haven't addressed, you haven't earned the position. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Yue-2025|Yue #8]].
> 6. **Identical sub-section shape across domains.** When proving a single qualitative claim on multiple domains (math/code/vision), use identical sub-section structure. Readers compare across domains by position. Cited: [[Writing-Best-Practices-Yue-2025|Yue #6]].

## 7. Cross-cutting techniques observed

Synthesis of the *Cross-cutting techniques* sections of each per-paper note:

- **Typography discipline:** Universally observed. Every paper uses bold for named entities/brands, italics for thesis/findings/research-questions, and bold mini-headings inside paragraphs. The 3-channel system is non-negotiable.
- **Caption discipline:** Universal — captions end with a claim, not a legend. The strongest implementations are Hivemind (Fig 1 with verbatim outputs) and Wang (bolded finding-sentence opens every caption).
- **Number anchoring:** Universal. 5-7 anchor numbers repeated verbatim across abstract, intro, sections, and conclusion. The strongest implementation is Chase's $\sqrt{d}$ which appears even in the title's "Optimal" claim.
- **Hedging discipline:** Universal, with subtle variants. Direct verbs for measurements; modal verbs for causes/mechanisms/extrapolations. Bonnaire's *"behaviour likely rooted in the spectral bias"* is exemplary; Qiu's *"Although the exact causes remain unclear due to proprietary training details, possible explanations include..."* is the clearest didactic case.

## 8. Top 12 universal rules (TL;DR of the TL;DR)

If you can only remember 12 things from this 11-paper synthesis:

> [!success] The cross-genre playbook (compressed)
> 1. **Hedge causes, not measurements.** (Lipton)
> 2. **Captions are claims, not legends.**
> 3. **Anchor 5-7 numbers, repeat verbatim.**
> 4. **Title encodes the contribution.**
> 5. **Two scientific legs on one artifact.**
> 6. **Steelman objections inside the section.**
> 7. **Open abstract at the problem, not the field.**
> 8. **Italicise / quote the thesis sentence.**
> 9. **Section headers are claims, not topics.**
> 10. **Conclusion ≤ 25 lines, no new content.**
> 11. **Negative results / "didn't work" disclosures buy credibility.**
> 12. **Appendix as reviewer insurance — every body claim has an appendix slot.**

Then look up §5 for the genre-specific playbook for the paper you're writing.
